> ABSTRACT 14
> The flow and density field characteristics around a bubble curtain in a laboratory scale lock-exchange setup are 15
> investigated using two-phase large-eddy simulations. We study the detailed hydrodynamics and show that there are 16
> three qualitatively distinct (sub)regimes within the previously classified breakthrough regime. The occurrence of 17
> these regimes depends not only on air Froude number that characterises the relative strength of the bubble curtain 18
> and the gravity current, but also on an additional non-dimensional parameter: the density ratio between the salt and 19
> fresh water. The dependence on this additional parameter is also observed in how effective bubble curtains are in 20
> blocking the transport of salt to the fresh part of the lock. Hence, it has important implications for the optimisation 21
> of bubble curtains in ship locks. 22

Keywords: Bubble curtains; Lock-exchange flow; Salt intrusion; Ship locks; Euler-Euler two-fluid modelling; 23

1 Introduction 25 

Bubble plumes are multiphase buoyant plumes that have long been of relevance for a wide range of 26 

engineering applications (Boufadel et al., 2020; Brevik and Kristiansen, 2002; Dissanayake et al., 27 

2018). They are commonly used in bubble columns and air-lift reactors due to their ability to 28 

enhance mixing and mass transfer (Chisti and Moo-Young, 1987; Kantarci et al., 2005). When in-29 

dividual bubble plumes are placed on a line they form a bubble curtain or screen, which can enhance 30 

mixing or act as a barrier between two fluids with different properties. As a result, bubble curtains 31 

have gained a lot of interest as a solution to various environmental flow problems. For example, they 32 

can be used for underwater noise mitigation (W¨ ursig et al., 2000), lake destratification/aeration 33 

(Li and Liu, 2025; Murai et al., 2025; Schladow, 1993; Wen and Torrest, 1987), barriers trapping 34 

oil and sediments (Covarrubias-Contreras et al., 2025; Cutroneo et al., 2014; Dugu´ e et al., 2015; 35 

Liu et al., 2025; McClimans et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2024), controlling ichthyoplankton dispersion 36 

(Prasad et al., 2026), and preventing intrusion of sea water in ship locks (Abraham et al., 1973; 37 

Bacot et al., 2022; Keetels et al., 2011; Oldeman et al., 2020; O’Mahoney et al., 2024). The latter 38 

is the focus of this paper. 39 

When the gate of the ship lock that separates the dense seawater and the fresh river water is 40 

opened for the ships to pass through, due to the barotropic pressure gradient, a lock-exchange flow 41 

occurs. In this flow, the dense salt water flows beneath the fresh water as a gravity or density 42 

current (Benjamin, 1968; Shin et al., 2004), causing the intrusion of salt water into the rivers. 43 

With increasing sea level and severity of droughts, the problem of salt intrusion demands more 44 

measures to mitigate salinisation of fresh water reserves (Lee et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025). Bubble 45 

curtains are a promising choice for mitigation because the vertical momentum of the bubbly flow 46 

can effectively impede this infiltration of saltwater. In fact, bubble curtains are already installed 47 

and working in several ship locks in the Netherlands and more are being planned. However, several 48 

questions remain about the fluid dynamics of bubble curtains to optimize their use. 49 

The earliest known studies on the topic are the seminal works of Abraham and Van der Burgh 50 

(1962) and Abraham et al. (1973) who laid down the fundamental theory and concepts behind 51 

the hydrodynamics and performance of bubble curtains in lock-exchange configurations by means 52 

of theoretical modelling and field measurements. They showed that the amount of saltwater that 53 

infiltrates the bubble curtain depends mainly on the air Froude number (Fr air ), which describes 54 

the relative strength of the bubble curtain to the gravity current. 55 

Interest in this topic has seen a resurgence over the past 15 years. With advances in optical mea-56 

surement techniques and computational fluid dynamics (CFD), various studies have contributed 57

Bacot et al. (2022) developed a semi-analytical model in the curtain-driven regime using steady-73 

state mass conservation to compute the effectiveness. Raaghav et al. (2025) showed, by means 74 

of numerical simulations and an unsteady semi-analytical model, that the time elapsed after the 75

curtain in the curtain-driven regime. O’Mahoney et al. (2024) investigated the effect of the bubble 77 

size on flow characteristics and effectiveness. They reported that larger bubbles are more effective 78 

in keeping the salt and fresh halves separate and observed that the optimal value of Fr air increased 79 

for larger bubbles. They attributed this dependence to the influence of bubble size on entrainment 80 

by the bubble curtain. 81

2 Problem setup and definition of the parameters 102 

We represent the lock (shown in Figure 2) in an idealised rectangular tank of size L × Hd × W in 103 

the x, y, and z directions, respectively (with y the vertical direction). The origin of the reference 104 

frame is located at the centre of the tank in the horizontal and at the bottom of the tank in the 105 

vertical. The lock gate, located at x = 0, separates two halves of the tank such that each half has a 106 

length L/ 2 filled with water of different density up to a depth H. Throughout the paper constant 107 

values of L = 6 m, Hd = 0 .2 m, H = 0 .15 m and W = 0 .2 m were used. A porous sparger is 108 

located on the tank floor ( y = 0) at the location of the lock gate ( x = 0) and spans the tank in 109 

the z-direction (i.e. along the width). In the x-direction, it has a width ds = 0 .02 m, which means 110 

that it is located at −ds/2 ≤ x ≤ ds/2 and −W/ 2 ≤ z ≤ W/ 2. The sparger delivers an air volume 111 

flow rate per unit width qair to form the line bubble plume. The bubbles are assumed to have a 112 

uniform bubble diameter db = 2 mm. 113

Determining the effectiveness Eend of bubble curtains as a function of the parameters of the 144 

problem is a crucial question for their operation. Most previous studies extensively investigated 145 

the dependence of the effectiveness on Fr air while keeping other parameters constant or ignoring 146 

their effects. For example, Oldeman et al. (2020), Bacot et al. (2022) and O’Mahoney et al. (2024) 147 

kept σ −1 = 0 .020, a typical value of the density difference between sea water and fresh water, while 148 

Bacot et al. (2022) also considered cases with σ − 1 > 0.020 even up to σ − 1 ≈ 0.187 that extend 149 

beyond the limits where the Boussinesq approximation is valid. However, the density difference 150 

between the water in the lock chamber and the harbours can be much smaller ( σ − 1 ≲ 0.010), 151 

since the lock chamber mostly contains brackish water (Bakker et al., 2026; Weiler et al., 2026). 152 

Then, it can be seen from the definition of ˆ qair in Equation (2) that, for Fr air ≲ 1 and σ −1 ≈ 0.010, 153 

the value of ˆ qair ≤ 1 × 10 −3. Previous studies show that the entrainment depends on ˆ qair (Fanneløp 154 

et al., 1991; Kobus, 1968). From the work of Fanneløp et al. (1991), it can be inferred that, around 155 

the value of ˆ qair ≈ 1.6 × 10 −3, the entrainment by the plume become constant. In this study, we 156 

will show that this has important implications for the effectiveness of bubble curtains. 157

both sides. The lack of a surface current for x < 0 is due to the strength of the exchange flow near 262 

the surface in the positive x-direction. 263 

Quite remarkably, in this regime, some differences are observed between the case with σ − 1 = 264 

0.008 and that with σ − 1 = 0 .028. On the one hand, the case with σ − 1 = 0 .008 shows less 265 

dilution and, on the other hand, the recirculation cell is slightly smaller. The differences between 266 

simulations with different values of σ will be discussed in more detail in the following. 267

4.3 Curtain-driven onset regime 268 

As Fr air increases further, we observe further dilution in the gravity currents due to the increased 269 

mixing around the bubble curtain (see Figures 3e and f). Furthermore, the streamlines displayed 270 

in Figures 4e and f show, in addition to a recirculation cell for x > 0, also a small recirculation 271 

cell and surface currents for x < 0. The simulation for σ − 1 = 0 .008 shows a smaller recirculation 272 

cell than that for σ − 1 = 0 .028. In the curtain-driven regime, the recirculation cells on both sides 273 

of the bubble curtain are approximately equal in strength and size (Bacot et al., 2022; Raaghav 274 

et al., 2025). However, in this onset regime, even if the curtain has enough inertia to form surface 275 

currents and recirculation cells on both sides of the bubble curtain, they are strongly asymmetric. 276 

Hence, we denote this regime as the curtain-driven onset regime. 277 

The asymmetry between both sides is due to the fluid parcels in the surface current for x < 0278 

sinking more quickly than those for x > 0. In fact, the end of the recirculation cell for x > 0 is not 279 

observed within the spatial range plotted in Figure 4. Interestingly, Abraham et al. (1973) sketched 280 

a similar flow with a small recirculation cell for x < 0 based on surface flow observations. However, 281 

the recirculation cell for x > 0 was not considered, probably because its signature on the surface 282 

is not as clear. 283 

The gradual appearance and growth of the recirculation cell for x < 0 with Fr air shows that there 284 

is not a sharp transition between the breakthrough and the curtain-driven regime at Fr air ≈ 0.9 (as 285 

proposed by Bacot et al., 2022). Instead, we observe a smooth transition through the curtain-driven 286 

onset regime. 287 

5 Quantitative description 288 

In the previous section, we qualitatively introduced three sub-regimes: total breakthrough , diluted 289 

breakthrough , and curtain-driven onset . In what follows, we provide a quantitative characterisation 290 

to more precisely capture the transitions between these sub-regimes and delineate their respective 291 

limits. 292 

5.1 Approach 293 

We aim to quantitatively describe how key characteristics of the flow and density fields change as a 294 

function of Fr air and σ − 1. We focus on the entrainment by the bubble curtain, the dilution by the 295

we define a small rectangular control volume around the bubble curtain. However, one difficulty 297

6 Discussion 499 

For practical applications, it is desired to operate bubble curtains close to the optimum or at 500 

slightly lower Fr air values to require less energy without a major loss in effectiveness. Hence, it is 501 

important to understand how the effectiveness and its optimal value depend on the parameters of 502 

the problem. From the early work of Abraham and Van der Burgh (1962) to the studies of Oldeman 503 

et al. (2020), Bacot et al. (2022), and O’Mahoney et al. (2024), the effectiveness of bubble curtains 504 

for reducing salt intrusion has been considered exclusively as a function of Fr air . However, our 505 

results show that the effectiveness is also a function of an additional parameter (either σ − 1 or 506 

ˆqair ). This is of importance for small values of Fr air (i.e. in the breakthrough regime) because, in 507 

this range, low values of σ translate into low values of ˆ qair , implying that the entrainment coefficient 508 

of the bubble curtain is not constant but highly dependent on ˆ qair .509

and similar values have been used in recent experimental and numerical studies (Bacot et al., 2022; 511 

Oldeman et al., 2020; O’Mahoney et al., 2024; Raaghav et al., 2025). However, in ship locks, σ − 1512 

tends to be a factor of two smaller ( σ − 1 ≈ 0.010) (Bakker et al., 2026; Weiler et al., 2026). Our 513 

results show that, for such low values of σ − 1, the effectiveness in the breakthrough regime can be 514 

up to 20% smaller and that the maximum effectiveness occurs for higher values of Fr air compared 515 

to situations where σ −1 ≳ 0.020. These results suggest that bubble curtains in locks further inland 516 

where σ − 1 might have much lower values would be less effective and optimum effectiveness would 517 

occur at higher Fr air values. 518 

In general, the dependence of the entrainment coefficient on ˆ qair in the curtain-driven regime is of 519 

secondary importance. Raaghav et al. (2025) completely neglected this dependence by considering 520 

only simulations with ˆ qair > 4 × 10 −3. Even for the case with σ − 1 = 0 .010, the entrainment 521 

coefficient becomes constant (i.e. ˆ qair > 2 × 10 −3) for Fr air ≈ 1.25, barely into the range of Fr air 522 

values considered by Raaghav et al. (2025). However, it is important to consider this dependence 523 

when interested in optimal effectiveness (Fr air ≈ 1) or if the value of σ − 1 is even smaller. 524 

Furthermore, our results show that the flow in the breakthrough regime displays important qual-525 

itative differences for different values of the control parameters. We describe three qualitatively 526 

different regimes dependent on two control parameters: Fr air and either σ − 1 or ˆ qair . Transitions 527 

between different regimes occurred at different Fr air values because the entrainment by the bubble 528 

plume depends on ˆ qair . Previous work proposing theoretical estimates for the effectiveness as a 529 

function of Fr air (see e.g. Abraham et al., 1973; Bacot et al., 2022) has not considered the depen-530 

dence of the entrainment coefficient on ˆ qair . For example, Bacot et al. (2022) used the constant 531 

value proposed by Paillat and Kaminski (2014). Abraham et al. (1973) used a range of values for 532 

the entrainment coefficient but considered it independent of Fr air . In addition, to build the theo-533 

retical model, Abraham et al. (1973) used the flow characteristics based on the results of Bulson 534 

(1961) in the case of homogeneous water. However, our results show that the flow structure in the 535 

breakthrough regime (e.g., the size of the recirculation cells and the height of the surface currents) 536 

varies with the parameters of the problem. Hence, we expect that the detailed new knowledge 537 

provided by our simulations can be used to develop new analytical models for the effectiveness of 538 

bubble curtains in the breakthrough regime. 539

7 Conclusions 540 

We performed large-eddy simulations of a bubble curtain in a lock-exchange configuration for 541 

Fr air ≲ 1. This range covers the breakthrough regime, extending into the onset of the curtain-542 

driven regime. We report new insights into the flow characteristics and show that what has been 543 

deemed the “breakthrough regime” by Bacot et al. (2022) is composed of qualitatively different 544 

sub-regimes. We refer to these as the total breakthrough , the diluted breakthrough , and the curtain-545 

driven onset regimes (in order of increasing Fr air values). 546 

In the total breakthrough regime, the exchange flow is nearly unaffected by the presence of the 547 

bubble curtain. In the diluted breakthrough regime, the bubble curtain entrains the salt water 548 

from the bottom, and a large recirculation cell is formed on the saltwater side of the bubble screen. 549 

Hence, salt and fresh water are mixed, and the gravity current emanating from the bubble curtain 550 

on the freshwater side of it has an intermediate density. Finally, in the curtain-driven onset regime, 551 

an additional recirculation cell appears on the freshwater side of the curtain, and salt water is 552 

entrained by the curtain all the way to the surface. 553 

Importantly, the transitions between these regimes do not depend only on Fr air , but also on 554 

an additional non-dimensional parameter: either σ − 1 or ˆ qair (only two of the three parameters 555 

are independent). In the present work, we thoroughly examined the entrainment characteristics 556 

of the curtain and its dependence on ˆ qair . To our knowledge, this is the first study showing the 557 

asymptotic behaviour of the entrainment coefficient for line bubble plumes as a function of ˆ qair and 558 

allowing to estimate the critical value of ˆ qair ≈ 2 × 10 −3 beyond which the entrainment coefficient 559 

becomes a constant. Because the entrainment coefficient depends on ˆ qair , when ˆ qair ≲ 2 × 10 −3 the 560 

effectiveness of bubble curtains is controlled by both Fr air and ˆ qair . This has consequences for their 561 

application to mitigate saltwater intrusion. 562
